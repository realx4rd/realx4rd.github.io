## The module in one sentence

By the end of this week you will have a pricing floor you can defend, a proposal that
opens with the client's problem, a contract that protects you before the first hour of
work, and a dated plan to land your first paid gig.

## What we're really learning

Most new freelancers lose money before they earn it — not because they lack skill but
because they skip the business layer. They undercharge (because they're nervous), start
work without a deposit (because it feels awkward to ask), and scope projects loosely
(because they don't want to seem difficult). Every one of those habits is fixable with
a template and a script you've rehearsed once.

The big mindset shift: **you are not asking for permission to be paid.** You are
offering a service with a clear price, clear terms, and a clear outcome. Clients who
respect good work expect these things — the ones who push back on a basic contract or
a reasonable deposit are telling you something important early, while it's still cheap
to walk away.

## Why underpricing is the real trap

It feels safe to start low. It isn't. Low prices attract price-sensitive clients who
micromanage, move slowly, and rarely refer you to anyone. They also train you to believe
your work isn't worth more. **Your floor protects your business and your sanity.** Set it
with the true-rate math on Day 1 and hold it — then raise it after every two or three
projects, before you feel ready, as the SelfEmployed resource explains.

## This is the finale — you go pro here

Everything in the freelancer-foundation track has been preparation for this week.
You've learned the technical skills, built projects, and shipped things. Now you learn
how to get paid for them. The launch kit you assemble in the project is not a school
exercise — it's the actual document you'll open the next time a lead comes in. Keep it,
refine it with experience, and revisit it every time you raise your rates.

> **One concrete action beats ten plans.** The 30-day plan on Day 5 has a hard deadline
> because plans without dates are wishes. Put a real date on sending your first proposal
> — and then send it.
