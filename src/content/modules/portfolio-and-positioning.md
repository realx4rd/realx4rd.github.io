## The module in one sentence

This week you stop describing yourself as a developer and start describing yourself as **the person who solves a specific problem for a specific kind of client** — and you build the assets that make that claim believable.

## Why generalists struggle (and how to fix it)

"I do web development" is the freelance equivalent of a restaurant with a menu that lists every cuisine on earth. It might be technically true, but it doesn't help anyone decide to walk in.

Clients hire on **trust and fit**, and specialists signal both. When a client with a SaaS product that needs a redesign finds a developer whose positioning says "I help SaaS founders redesign their apps to reduce churn," the decision is easy. The same developer, presenting as a generalist, has to rebuild that trust from scratch in every conversation.

Picking a niche does not mean you will never take other work. It means you will attract the right work **without having to chase it**, and you will charge more for it because you are no longer competing on price with every other developer in the world.

## The portfolio problem nobody talks about

Most developer portfolios are feature lists dressed up in screenshots. They answer the question "what did you build?" when clients are asking "what will change if I hire you?"

Case studies flip the frame. A case study says: here was a problem a real person or organisation had, here is exactly what I did about it, and here is what was different afterwards. That structure — **Problem → Action → Result** — is what separates a portfolio that wins clients from a portfolio that just proves you can code.

If you don't have client work yet, personal and practice projects still work. What matters is that you describe your own reasoning, the decisions you made, and what you learned — not just a list of technologies used.

## Consistency is the signal

By the end of this module you will have three things pointing at the same niche: your positioning statement, your portfolio or about page, and your GitHub and LinkedIn bios. That consistency is not just aesthetic — it tells a client that you know exactly who you are and who you are for. That is a form of confidence that shows up before you say a single word in a sales conversation.
